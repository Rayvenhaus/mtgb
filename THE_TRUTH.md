---

In the beginning, there was filament.

And the filament was good, and it fed through the hotend at precisely the right temperature, and the first layer adhered to the build plate with the quiet confidence of something that had absolutely no idea what was about to happen to it.

The build plate, for its part, had been carefully levelled. Levelled again. Levelled a third time using a different method found in a forum post from 2019, which contradicted the first two methods but was written with such conviction that it had to be right. The bed adhesion had been applied. The correct bed adhesion. Not the other bed adhesion — the one that worked perfectly until it didn't, and then spectacularly didn't for three prints in a row before anyone admitted that perhaps the formula had changed and the universe was, as usual, having a bit of a laugh.

The first layer went down perfectly.

This is always the most dangerous moment in 3D printing. The first layer going down perfectly is the universe's way of saying *watch this*.

---

And then, somewhere around hour six of an eight-hour print, while the operator was elsewhere in the most modern sense of the word — present at the computer, physically there, hands on keyboard, eyes on screen, but elsewhere in every way that mattered — everything went wrong.

Not dramatically wrong. Not the kind of wrong that announces itself with smoke and the acrid perfume of regret and melted PLA. Not the heroic wrong, the noble wrong, the wrong that you can at least tell as a story at a 3D printing meetup and have people nod with the deep understanding of the initiated.

No.

The quiet wrong. The insidious wrong. The wrong that happens so gradually and so politely that by the time you notice it, it has been wrong for quite some time and has settled in, made itself comfortable, and begun to regard itself as the normal state of affairs.

The kind of wrong that results in what the community has come to call, with the weary affection of people who have been there many times and will be there many times again and have made a kind of peace with this fact, *spaghetti*.

Not the edible kind.

The other kind.

The kind that means you've been sitting at your computer; your very capable, entirely connected, fully operational computer, the same computer that is directly responsible for telling the printer what to do in the first place — while the actual print detached itself from the build plate, shrugged in a philosophical manner that honestly deserves more respect than it gets, and began expressing itself in free-form abstract sculpture three centimetres above where it was supposed to be.

The kind that, when you eventually glance away from whatever has consumed the last two hours of your attention; the forum thread, the YouTube rabbit hole, the game that was supposed to be one quick match, the code that was supposed to be one small fix, the scroll that had no particular destination and reached it anyway — and remember, with the sudden cold clarity of someone who has been here before, that there is a printer.

That the printer has been printing.

That you haven't checked on the printer in a while.

That you probably should have checked on the printer.

And you turn around, or you switch tabs, or you just look slightly to the left where the printer lives, and you see:

*Oh.*

Not a question. Not an exclamation. Just *oh.* The single syllable of someone who has moved past surprise and through understanding and arrived at the flat, philosophical acceptance of a person who knew, somewhere in the back of their mind, that this was always a possibility, and had chosen, in the way humans always choose, to believe it probably wouldn't happen this time.

It happened this time.

---

There was a phone. The phone was face-down on the desk next to the keyboard — not away, not in another room, not lost down the back of the sofa, but right there, present, within arm's reach, face-down in the way that phones go face-down when their owner has decided, consciously or otherwise, that they are not to be consulted for the foreseeable future because there is something more pressing on the screen in front of them.

There was a SimplyPrint browser tab.

The SimplyPrint browser tab was there. It would have shown the status perfectly clearly, in real time, with colour coding and percentages and everything a reasonable person could want from a print monitoring interface.

It was behind forty-seven other browser tabs.

Not forty-seven as a figure of speech, or forty-seven as a comfortable exaggeration deployed for comedic effect. Forty-seven as a count. As a genuine archaeological record of an evening's browsing, each tab a sedimentary layer of intention and distraction and the fundamental human inability to close things that might, in some hypothetical future moment, be needed.

Three of them were the same Reddit thread, opened at different points in the evening as memory and attention drifted and returned and drifted again. One was a Wikipedia article about something completely unrelated that had been open since Tuesday and would never be read but also would never quite be closed, because what if you needed it. One was a YouTube video, paused at the four-minute mark, waiting with the patient resignation of a YouTube video that knows it will never be resumed.

The SimplyPrint tab was in there somewhere.
Technically accessible.
Practically invisible.
Buried under the accumulated weight of an evening spent being present at a computer and absent from the print.

The computer itself — the very machine running the slicer, hosting the files, connected to the printer, possessed of every piece of information required to know that something had gone wrong — sat in magnificent, well-connected, perfectly-informed silence and said nothing.

Because nothing had asked it to say anything.

Because there was no machine that went Bing.

Not yet.

---

The printer knew.

The printer had known for quite some time. The printer had, in fact, been aware of the situation from approximately the moment it began, and had logged it internally, and had updated its status in perfect, faithful, completely accurate detail — printing, printing, printing, *error*, error, error, error, error — a meticulous record of exactly how and when everything had gone wrong, available to anyone who thought to look.

No one had thought to look.

The printer, in the manner of printers everywhere, had chosen to keep this information to itself. It had the data. It knew the situation. It maintained its position on the desk with the serene composure of a machine that has fulfilled its contractual obligations and considers what you do with that information to be entirely your own affair.

This was considered by all to be suboptimal.

---

The problem, when examined carefully and with the benefit of hindsight and the cold clarity of a person who has been here before and will, statistically, be here again, was not the printer.

The printer was doing exactly what the printer was supposed to do, which was print things and, when printing things became impossible, carefully document its inability to print things and wait with mechanical patience for someone to notice.

The problem was the gap.

The gap between the printer knowing and the human knowing. The gap between *something has gone wrong* and *someone has been informed*. The gap that existed not because the human was away — they were right there, at the desk, at the computer, present in every physical sense — but because being present at a computer and paying attention to the right thing at the right moment are not, it turns out, the same thing.

The gap that was, in practice, measured not in distance but in browser tabs.

In the natural human tendency to mean to check the printer and then not check the printer and then mean to check it again and then get distracted by something and then remember and then check and find that sixty-three percent from forty minutes ago is still sixty-three percent now and has been sixty-three percent for longer than sixty-three percent has any right to be.

There is a technical term for this approach to print monitoring.

The technical term is *hoping*.

---

In the words of the great philosopher-kings of Discworld, there are those who, when confronted with a problem, ask "why?" And there are those who ask "why not?" And then there are engineers, who ask "how do I make it go Bing when something breaks?"

Pratchett understood this distinction completely. His witches didn't ask why the magic worked. They asked what needed doing and then did it, and if the doing of it involved a certain amount of headology and creative 
interpretation of the rules of causality, well, the important thing was that it worked.

His wizards, by contrast, spent a great deal of time asking why, and writing papers about why, and arguing about the papers, and forming committees to review the arguments, and then funding further research into why the committees had reached the conclusions they had, and by the time any of this was resolved, the problem had either solved itself, gotten significantly worse, or been inherited by a new generation of wizards who would approach it with fresh eyes and the same fundamental commitment to not actually doing anything about it.

MTGB is for the engineers.

MTGB is for the people who looked at the gap — the gap between the printer knowing and the human knowing, the gap between the error and the awareness, the gap that is filled, in practice, with spaghetti and the quiet philosophical devastation of a failed print discovered too late — and said:

*Right. Let's fix that.*

Not with a committee. Not with a paper. Not with a lengthy discussion about the fundamental nature of notification architecture and the epistemological implications of real-time versus polling-based event delivery, though there was, if we're being honest, a certain amount of that as well, mostly in the design documents and largely to make the architecture diagrams look more impressive.

With a small application.
That lives in the system tray.
That watches your printers.
That notices when things go wrong.
And goes Bing.

Into the computer you are already sitting at.
Into the screen you are already looking at.
Past the forty-seven browser tabs.
Past the face-down phone.
Past the second monitor you meant to glance at.
Past the good intentions and the optimistic assumptions 
and the hope that everything is probably fine.

Directly to you.
Right now.
While you can still do something about it.

---

The name comes, as the best names do, from a moment of complete clarity that arrived approximately forty-five minutes into a conversation about what to call the thing.

Monty Python's "The Machine That Goes Ping" is, on the surface, a sketch about a hospital. It is also, if you look at it sideways in the right light — the way the Monty Python crew looked at things sideways in the right light and saw the truth of them — a sketch about every piece of technology ever commissioned by a committee.

The machine has one function.
It goes ping.
It is enormously expensive.
It is enormously impressive.
Nobody questions it.
This is, perhaps, the most realistic thing ever filmed.

MTGB goes Bing.
This is what it does.
This is all it does.
It is not enormously expensive — it is, in fact, free.
It is not enormously impressive — it is, in fact, a small 
application that sits in the corner of your screen and 
watches.

But when your print fails at two in the morning, or finishes after eight hours of anxious, distracted waiting, or drops offline with no warning, or crawls past seventy-five percent completion on the job you were beginning to think would never end...

It goes Bing.

Not into the void.
Not onto the face-down phone.
Not into the browser tab that is technically there but 
practically invisible behind the forty-six others.

Into your screen.
Right now.
You know.

And in that moment, the small application in the corner of your screen is the most important piece of software on your computer.

---

There is a concept in Pratchett's work — in all the best work, really — of the thing that is too important to be taken seriously, and too absurd to be dismissed. The Disc runs on stories. Death rides a pale horse and has opinions about cats. The most powerful magic is often the most mundane, because the mundane is what actually touches people's lives.

A notification app is a mundane thing.

A notification app that watches your 3D printer while you are right there at your computer being gloriously, productively, completely distracted by something else entirely — that sits in the brass and mahogany dignity of its Art Deco styling and monitors your print farm with the patient gravity of a Victorian instrument designed for a purpose no Victorian would have understood — that goes Bing in the middle of your doom scrolling, your forum browsing, your one-quick-match gaming session, your one-small-fix coding session, your late-night everything-session — with the calm authority of something that has always known this was going to happen and has been waiting, patiently, to tell you —

That is, in its way, a small magic.

Not the dramatic kind. Not the kind with lightning and prophecy and dark lords and the fate of the world.

The quiet kind.
The useful kind.
The kind that makes things slightly better for the people 
who need it, right at the moment they need it, without 
asking them to remember to look.

The best kind.

---

MTGB was built for the SimplyPrint community.

For the print farm operators running fifteen printers and the quiet, persistent, entirely rational prayer that none of them fail simultaneously at two in the morning on a Thursday when there are orders due Friday and the emergency filament is still in transit from wherever emergency filament comes from, and there are forty-seven browser tabs open, and the phone is face-down, and nobody is watching.

For the bedroom hobbyist who has named their printer. Not ironically named it, or named it as a joke, but actually named it, given it a personality in their mind, possibly apologised to it on at least one occasion when  something went wrong that was honestly probably their fault and the printer deserved better.

For the person who has strong opinions about bed adhesion that they will share with anyone who stays still long enough, and who has tried approximately eleven different approaches and has a preferred one, and the preferred one works until it doesn't, and when it doesn't they try a twelfth approach and add it to the list.

For the school lab technician who is responsible for thirty machines, twenty-eight students of varying enthusiasm and competence, one persistent smell of hot plastic that has been there since October and is now considered part of the room's character, and the daily philosophical question of whether "it's printing but it doesn't look right" is a crisis requiring immediate intervention or simply one of those things.

For the person who has a second monitor, or a specific corner of their screen designated for the SimplyPrint tab, and checks it every twenty minutes or so, or means to check it every twenty minutes, or checks it whenever they remember, which averages out to every twenty minutes across the evening but is in practice distributed as forty-five minutes of forgetting followed by five minutes of anxious checking followed by the relief of seeing it's still printing followed by the gradual drift back into forgetting.

For the person who switches to the SimplyPrint tab, sees it's at sixty-three percent, thinks *good*, switches back to whatever they were doing, and doesn't switch back for another hour, at which point it is either at  ninety-one percent and fine or it has been at sixty-three percent for the last forty-five minutes and is not fine, and the difference between these two outcomes is not visible from the other tab, where everything looks exactly the same regardless of what the printer is doing.

For everyone who has ever thought, while deep in something else entirely, *I should check the printer*, and then not checked the printer, and then thought it again ten minutes later, and then not checked again, and then eventually checked, and found that they should have checked the first time they thought they should check the printer.

For the person standing at their desk at eleven-thirty at night, looking at what their printer has produced in the last two hours while they were sitting right there not looking at it, having that quiet, flat, philosophical moment that all 3D printer operators eventually reach — the moment beyond surprise, beyond frustration, beyond the five stages of print failure and into something that might, in the right light, almost resemble acceptance — and thinking:

*If only something had told me.*

*While I was right here.*

*While I could still have done something about it.*

This app is the something that tells you.
While you are right there.
While you can still do something about it.

---

It is free.
It is open source.
It will remain both of these things unconditionally, without asterisks, without "free tier" and "pro tier" and "enterprise tier" and the quiet creeping expansion of what "pro tier" means over successive quarterly updates.

It is free because the SimplyPrint community is made of people who share their designs and their profiles and their hard-won knowledge about bed adhesion freely and without hesitation, and an app built for that community should behave accordingly.

It is open source because transparency matters, especially in software that handles your credentials and watches your systems, and because Double-Secret Validation™ — which is what we call cryptography around here, because if we get it wrong someone is updating their CV at three in the morning, which is worse than finding spaghetti — should be visible to anyone who wants to check that we've done it properly.

We have done it properly.
The Redundant Department of Redundancy has reviewed it.
Twice.
In triplicate.

---

It monitors your prints.
It goes Bing.
It has, technically, no other purpose.

It is, nonetheless, completely and utterly essential.

You are already at your computer.
You are already at your screen.
You just need something to tell you when to look.

MTGB is that something.

The machine that goes Bing is watching.
It knows.
It will tell you.

Right now.
While you can still do something about it.

Never leave a print behind.
