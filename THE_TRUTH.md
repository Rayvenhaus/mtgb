# THE TRUTH OF THE BEGINNING AS TOLD BY THE COMMITTEE FOR THE REDUCTION OF FILAMENT WASTE
## As Filed with the Redundant Department of Redundancy
### Which was originally filed correctly, then filed again for safety.
### Subsequently filed a third time because the first filing was placed in the folder for Incorrectly Correct Filings and something ate the second filing.

---

In the beginning, there was filament.

The filament was good. It fed cleanly, melted properly, and laid down the first layer with that quiet, treacherous perfection every 3D printer operator eventually learns to distrust.

Because a perfect first layer is not reassurance.

It is a warning.

It is the universe smiling pleasantly and saying, *watch this.*

The bed had been levelled, of course. Then levelled again. Then levelled a third time using a method discovered in a forum post from 2019 written with such absolute conviction that it briefly overruled both experience and physics. Adhesion had been applied. The correct adhesion. Not the other one. Not the one that works flawlessly until, without warning, it develops standards.

Everything was fine.

Until it wasn’t.

Not dramatically. No smoke. No fire. No noble plume of molten regret rising toward the ceiling like an offering to the ancient gods of poor timing.

This was the quieter kind of failure.

The sly kind.

The kind that slips in politely and, by the time you notice it, has already made itself comfortable.

The kind that ends in spaghetti.

Not the edible kind.

The other kind.

The kind where your print detaches, the nozzle carries on with complete confidence, and somewhere above the build plate your carefully engineered object abandons structure altogether and embraces abstract expressionism.

And the truly irritating part is this:

You were there.

At the desk. At the computer. Present in the strict biological sense. Hands on keyboard. Eyes on screen. Entirely available and absent in every way that mattered.

Because this is the real problem with modern making: you can be three feet from disaster and still miss it completely.

There was a phone on the desk, face-down.

There was a SimplyPrint tab open.

Well. *Open* in the archaeological sense. It was somewhere in the layered sediment of a browser containing forty-seven tabs, three of which were the same thread, one of which had been paused halfway through a video it would never finish, and one of which was a Wikipedia article no one was ever going to read but which had, through sheer stubbornness, become part of the machine’s permanent architecture.

The printer knew something was wrong.

It had known for some time.

It had logged the error, updated its status, and continued existing with the serene dignity unique to machines that consider awareness to be someone else’s department.

And the computer, glowing with all the connectivity and intelligence modern civilisation could muster, sat there in immaculate silence.

Not because it lacked the information.

Because nothing had told it to say anything.

There was no machine that went Bing.

Not yet.

And that was the problem.

Not the printer.

The gap.

The gap between the printer knowing and the human knowing.

The gap between *this has failed* and *someone should probably do something before the printer finishes recreating a nervous breakdown in PLA.*

That gap is where prints die.

It is measured in distraction, in browser tabs, in face-down phones, in “I’ll check it in a minute,” and in the deeply human habit of assuming that because something was fine the last time you looked, it has probably had the courtesy to remain fine.

This is not a monitoring strategy.

This is optimism in safety glasses.

So the question became very simple:

Why, if you are already at the computer, already at the desk, already staring into the bright electric maw of the machine for reasons entirely unrelated to your printer, should the printer’s crisis have to wait politely in another tab?

Why should failure require you to remember to go looking for it?

Why should the printer know before you do?

It shouldn’t.

So MTGB was born.

A small app. Sitting quietly in the system tray. Watching your printers. Waiting for the moment something matters: a failure, a disconnect, a finished print, the small but meaningful events that should not have to fight their way through forty-seven tabs and your increasingly optimistic attention span.

And then it goes Bing.

That’s it.

That’s the idea.

Not a sprawling monument to feature creep. Not a dashboard cathedral with six nested menus and a quarterly roadmap full of strategic nonsense. Just a simple little machine that notices when something important happens and tells you about it right now, on the computer you are already using, on the screen you are already looking at.

The name comes, as the best names do, from a moment of complete and immediate clarity.

Monty Python gave the world *The Machine That Goes Ping*.

This one goes Bing.

It is smaller, cheaper, and considerably more useful to anyone trying to stop a printer from quietly betraying them at 2:13 in the morning.

MTGB exists for the hobbyist with one printer and an unsafe amount of confidence.

For the farm operator with twelve printers and the thousand-yard stare of someone who no longer trusts silence.

For the school lab technician, the Etsy seller, the engineer, the tinkerer, the workshop goblin muttering about nozzle offsets, bed adhesion, and why this print really should have worked this time.

It exists for everyone who has ever thought:

*I should check the printer.*

And then didn’t.

And then discovered exactly why they should have.

It is free.

It is open source.

It will remain both, because software built for a community like this should behave like it belongs to one.

Also because transparency matters, especially in software that handles your credentials, watches your printers, and relies on **Double-Secret Validation™**, which is what we call cryptography when we are trying to remain cheerful about the fact that getting it wrong means someone is updating their CV at three in the morning.

So yes, the code is open.

The app is free.

The Bing is immediate.

And the point is simple:

You are already at the computer.

You are already at the screen.

You just need something to tell you when to look.

That’s MTGB.

A small machine that watches.

A small machine that knows.

A small machine that goes Bing while there is still time to do something useful about it.

Which, in a world held together by code, caffeine, wishful thinking, and half a roll of emergency filament, is about as close to practical magic as most of us are likely to get.

**Never leave a print behind.**
